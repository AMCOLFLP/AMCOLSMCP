2. Vocabulary List

Study the terms below. Focus on meaning, pronunciation, and how each word is used during real maritime operations.

TermStudent-Friendly MeaningHow It Is Used On Board
Head lineMooring line leading forward from the bow.Used to hold the vessel alongside and control forward movement.
Stern lineMooring line leading aft from the stern.Used to control aft movement.
Spring lineMooring line controlling forward or backward movement along the berth.Forward and aft springs are important during berthing.
Breast lineMooring line leading nearly perpendicular to the quay.Holds vessel close to berth.
Heave inPull a line in using winch or manpower.Bridge may order foredeck to heave in head line.
Slack awayPay out a line or reduce tension.Used when line is too tight or vessel needs movement.
Hold onStop heaving or slacking and hold the line.Used to freeze line movement immediately.
Make fastSecure a line to a bollard or bitt.Deck reports when line is made fast.
Let goRelease a line from bollard or ship.Used during departure or emergency release.
Snap-back zoneDanger area where a parted line may recoil.Crew must keep clear of snap-back zones.
WinchMachine used to heave or pay out mooring lines.Operated under command and with caution.
BollardStrong post on the quay for securing mooring lines.Line is passed to shore and made fast on bollard.
Hatch coverSteel cover for cargo hold.Must be secured before sea passage.
ManifoldConnection point for cargo hoses on tankers.Used during liquid cargo transfer.
StevedoresShore workers who load and discharge cargo.Coordinate with ship officers during cargo operations.
SWLSafe Working Load.Maximum safe load for equipment.
Read-backRepeating an order to confirm correct understanding.Required for safety-critical commands.
Closed-loop communicationSender gives order, receiver repeats it, sender confirms if needed.Used to prevent miscommunication during operations.

Vocabulary as operational control

Terms such as heave in, slack away, hold on, and make fast are not general verbs. They describe specific physical actions. Students should practise them with objects: head line, stern line, forward spring, aft spring, breast line, cargo hose, manifold valve, and hatch cover.
