Training Note

This module uses original classroom explanations and model conversations aligned with SMCP principles. It is designed to help students speak clearly, avoid informal language, and practise standard maritime communication patterns. Teachers should use the official IMO SMCP publication and school curriculum requirements as the final authority during formal certification training.

This textbook is written for students who need practical, easy-to-follow Maritime English for real bridge, port, and shipboard communication. Each unit gives vocabulary, grammar, message markers, standard phrase models, realistic conversations, and exercises. Study one unit at a time and practise speaking aloud with a partner.

Student-Friendly Reminder: SMCP is not about sounding impressive. It is about being clear, short, correct, and safe. In an emergency or busy port situation, simple standard phrases are better than long informal sentences.

Unit 1: VHF Fundamentals and Routine Traffic

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit teaches students how to establish contact, spell vessel information with the phonetic alphabet, conduct routine VHF exchanges, and perform a radio check using clear, standardized language. It is the foundation for safe communication with VTS, port control, pilot stations, and nearby vessels.

3. Grammar Focus

Imperatives and present simple are the main grammar points. Imperatives give short operational commands, such as “Stand by,” “Change to channel one-two,” and “Say again.” Present simple is used for facts, routine reports, and vessel particulars: “My draught is seven decimal five metres,” “I require pilot assistance,” and “The vessel carries containers.” Students should avoid long informal sentences and should speak in short message units.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Information: used to report facts such as position, draught, ETA, or traffic status.
  • Question: used to request specific information from VTS or another station.
  • Answer: used to give a direct response.
  • Request: used when asking permission or assistance.
  • Instruction: used by an authority such as VTS when giving an operational order.

5. Say This, Not That

Use the standard phrase in the middle column. The expressions in the last column may be understood in normal English, but they are too informal, vague, or risky for maritime operations.

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Vessel: Bangkok VTS, Bangkok VTS, this is Motor Vessel Ocean Pioneer, Ocean Pioneer. Call sign Hotel-Bravo-Seven-Two. Over.

VTS: Ocean Pioneer, this is Bangkok VTS. Send your message. Over.

Vessel: Information. I am entering the fairway from the south. My position is bearing one-eight-zero degrees, distance four decimal five miles from Buoy Bravo. My draught is seven decimal eight metres. Over.

VTS: Question. What is your ETA at the pilot station? Over.

Vessel: Answer. My ETA at the pilot station is one-six-three-zero local time. Request. Radio check on this channel. Over.

VTS: Answer. Readability is five. Instruction. Maintain listening watch on channel one-two. Report at Buoy Charlie. Over.

Vessel: Answer. Maintaining listening watch on channel one-two. Will report at Buoy Charlie. Out.

7. Core Feature: The Radio Check Protocol and 1-5 Readability Scale

A radio check must be short, professional, and made on an appropriate working channel. Do not occupy distress and safety channels for routine checks. The receiving station reports readability using a five-point scale: 1 = unreadable, 2 = readable now and then, 3 = readable with difficulty, 4 = readable, and 5 = perfectly readable. Students should avoid vague phrases such as “loud and clear” when a scale is required. A good radio check includes station called, station calling, the words “radio check,” and the response “readability is ...”.

Unit 2: Distress, Urgency, and Safety Communications

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit trains students to recognize and produce priority communications during emergencies. Students learn the difference between distress, urgency, and safety messages and practise organizing critical information under pressure.

3. Grammar Focus

Modals of obligation, necessity, and ability are essential in emergency communication. Use “must” for strict safety obligations, “must not” for prohibitions, “can/cannot” for capability, and “require” for assistance. Examples: “I cannot control flooding,” “You must proceed to muster station,” “I require immediate assistance,” and “You must not enter the engine room.” In distress traffic, accuracy is more important than complex grammar.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Warning: signals danger and urgency.
  • Request: asks for assistance or evacuation.
  • Information: reports position, nature of distress, and persons on board.
  • Instruction: gives life-saving orders.
  • Answer: confirms receipt and response.

5. Say This, Not That

Use the standard phrase in the middle column. The expressions in the last column may be understood in normal English, but they are too informal, vague, or risky for maritime operations.

SituationSay This - Standard SMCP StyleNot That - Colloquial / Risky English
Nature of distressInformation. I am on fire in engine room.Our engine room is burning badly.
Requesting helpRequest. I require immediate assistance.Please help us right now.
Loss of controlInformation. I am not under command.My ship cannot move properly.
Weather dangerWarning. Visibility restricted by fog.It is very foggy here.
Medical casePAN-PAN. Request medical assistance.We have a sick man and need a doctor.

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Vessel: MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. This is Motor Vessel Eastern Star, Eastern Star, Eastern Star. Call sign Lima-Delta-Four-Nine. MMSI two-three-five-six-seven-eight-nine-zero-one.

Vessel: MAYDAY Eastern Star. Position one-three degrees two-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees three-eight minutes East.

Vessel: Information. I have fire in engine room. Information. Fire is not under control. Request. I require immediate assistance.

Vessel: Information. Twenty-two persons on board. Intention. I am preparing to abandon ship if fire spreads to accommodation. Over.

Coast Radio: Eastern Star, this is Bangkok Radio. Answer. MAYDAY received. Instruction. Activate EPIRB. Instruction. Muster crew at emergency stations. SAR assistance is proceeding. Over.

Vessel: Answer. EPIRB activated. Crew mustered at emergency stations. Out.

7. Core Feature: The 3-Tier Alert System - MAYDAY, PAN-PAN, SECURITE

The priority of a maritime message must match the situation. MAYDAY is used only for distress: grave and imminent danger to life or the vessel. PAN-PAN is used for urgency: a serious situation affecting safety but not immediately life-threatening. SECURITE is used for safety: warnings about navigation or weather. The wrong priority can delay rescue, overload emergency channels, or create confusion. A complete distress message should include: distress signal, vessel identity, position, nature of distress, assistance required, persons on board, other useful information, and “Over.”

Unit 3: Pilotage and Port Arrival

This unit prepares students for port approach communication, pilot boarding arrangements, helm orders, engine orders, and safe coordination between bridge, pilot station, and deck crew.

3. Grammar Focus

Prepositions of place and movement are necessary for pilotage: “on the port side,” “alongside the berth,” “to the pilot station,” “one metre above the water,” “under the bridge,” and “ahead of the buoy.” Imperatives are also frequent in helm and engine orders. Students must use exact direction words: port, starboard, ahead, astern, midships, and degrees. Avoid “left” and “right” in formal navigation orders.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Instruction: used by pilots and bridge officers to give helm, engine, and boarding orders.
  • Question: used to ask boarding time, side, height, or tug requirements.
  • Answer: used to confirm arrangements.
  • Intention: used to state planned manoeuvre.
  • Information: used to report readiness or vessel condition.

5. Say This, Not That

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Vessel: Manila Pilot Station, this is Motor Vessel Sea Orchid. Call sign November-Kilo-Six-Five. Over.

Pilot Station: Sea Orchid, this is Manila Pilot Station. Question. What is your ETA at pilot boarding ground? Over.

Vessel: Answer. My ETA is zero-six-three-zero local time. Information. My present draught is eight decimal two metres. Request. Pilot boarding instructions. Over.

Pilot Station: Instruction. Proceed to pilot boarding ground Alpha. Instruction. Rig pilot ladder on starboard side, two metres above water. Advice. Make a lee for pilot boat. Over.

Vessel: Answer. Proceeding to pilot boarding ground Alpha. Pilot ladder will be rigged on starboard side two metres above water. I will make a lee for pilot boat. Out.

Pilot: Bridge, Pilot. Instruction. Port ten.

Helmsman: Port ten.

Pilot: Instruction. Midships.

Helmsman: Midships.

Pilot: Instruction. Dead slow ahead.

OOW: Dead slow ahead.

7. Core Feature: Standard Helm and Engine Orders

Helm and engine orders must be exact, short, and read back. A correct order-reply pattern prevents wrong-way turns, engine mistakes, and delayed reactions. Helm orders include “Port ten,” “Starboard twenty,” “Hard-a-port,” “Hard-a-starboard,” “Midships,” “Steady,” and “Steer zero-nine-zero.” Engine orders include “Dead slow ahead,” “Slow ahead,” “Half ahead,” “Full ahead,” “Stop engine,” “Dead slow astern,” and “Full astern.” The helmsman or officer repeats the order exactly. When the order is carried out, a confirmation may be given, such as “Course zero-nine-zero steadied.”

Unit 4: Vessel-to-Vessel Collision Avoidance

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit develops students’ ability to describe traffic situations, translate radar information into spoken reports, clarify intentions, and communicate without replacing COLREG responsibilities.

2. Vocabulary List

3. Grammar Focus

Future forms and conditional clauses are used to state intentions and expected actions: “I will alter course to starboard,” “I intend to pass astern of you,” and “If you maintain course and speed, I will pass astern.” However, VHF agreements must never replace the COLREGs. Students should state intentions clearly, avoid ambiguous words such as “left” and “right,” and use exact sea terms: port, starboard, ahead, astern, bearing, range, CPA, and TCPA.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Warning: used when risk of collision or danger exists.
  • Question: used to ask another vessel’s intentions.
  • Intention: used to declare own avoiding action.
  • Information: used to state observed traffic facts.
  • Advice: used when recommending a safer action.

5. Say This, Not That

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Vessel A: Motor Vessel Coral Sea, this is Motor Vessel Northern Light on your port bow. Over.

Vessel B: Northern Light, this is Coral Sea. Over.

Vessel A: Information. Your bearing is steady, range three decimal two miles. Warning. Risk of collision exists. Question. What are your intentions? Over.

Vessel B: Information. I am the give-way vessel. Intention. I will alter course to starboard and pass astern of you. Over.

Vessel A: Answer. You will alter course to starboard and pass astern of me. Information. I will maintain course and speed. Over.

Vessel B: Answer. Understood. I am altering course to starboard now. Out.

7. Core Feature: Radar-to-Speech Translation and COLREG Awareness

Radar information must be converted into precise spoken messages. A good traffic report includes target identity if known, bearing, range, CPA, TCPA, movement, and intended action. For example: “Information. Target on starboard bow, bearing zero-four-five degrees, range four decimal zero miles, CPA zero decimal three miles in twelve minutes.” This is clearer than “that ship is close.” Students must remember that VHF communication supports, but does not replace, COLREG duties. Action should be early, substantial, and clear.

3. Grammar Focus

Passive voice and present perfect are useful for reporting operational status. In mooring and cargo reports, the condition of equipment often matters more than the person who performed the action. Examples: “All lines are made fast,” “The hatch covers are secured,” “The cargo hose has been connected,” and “The forward spring has been slackened.” Imperatives are used for direct orders: “Heave in,” “Slack away,” “Hold on,” and “Keep clear.”

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Instruction: used for direct deck and cargo orders.
  • Answer: used to acknowledge or read back orders.
  • Information: used to report status or completion.
  • Warning: used for snap-back zones, suspended loads, or unsafe conditions.
  • Request: used when asking permission to start or stop work.

Cargo Control: Deck, Cargo Control. Question. Are hatch covers secured? Over.

Deck: Answer. Hatch covers are secured and checked. Out.

7. Core Feature: Closed-Loop Communication and Read-backs

Closed-loop communication has three stages: the sender gives a clear order, the receiver repeats the critical part, and the sender corrects or accepts the read-back. This prevents dangerous mistakes such as heaving the wrong line, starting a pump too early, or standing in an unsafe position. A weak reply such as “OK” is not enough for critical operations. A strong read-back repeats the action and object: “Slacking away the stern line,” “Stopping cargo pump number two,” or “Opening manifold valve number three.”

7. Core Feature: NAVTEX Decoding

NAVTEX messages are concise and may contain abbreviations. Students must learn to identify the type of message, the affected area, the position, the time, the hazard, and the recommended action. Common abbreviations include UTC, N, S, E, W, NM, VIS, WND, SEA, SWELL, GALE, and TEMPO. A student should convert a message such as “NAV WARN: UNLIT BUOY PSN 13-05N 100-48E. KEEP CLR.” into spoken English: “Warning. Unlit buoy reported in position one-three degrees zero-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees four-eight minutes East. Advice. Keep clear.”

Unit 7: Mastering All 8 SMCP Message Markers

Focus: This unit helps students understand, choose, and use all eight IMO SMCP message markers in real maritime communication. The purpose is to make spoken messages clear, short, and safe during routine operations, port arrival, emergency response, collision avoidance, onboard work, and navigational warnings.

Student-Friendly Key Idea: A message marker tells the listener the purpose of your message before the details are spoken. It is like a safety label for communication. When the marker is correct, the listener immediately knows whether the message is an Instruction, Advice, Warning, Information, Question, Answer, Request, or Intention.

Grammar Focus: How Markers Control Sentence Structure

SMCP markers are not grammar by themselves, but they guide the grammar that follows. After Instruction, the speaker normally uses an imperative verb: “Reduce speed,” “Alter course,” or “Report position.” After Advice, the speaker often uses “should” or a recommended action. After Warning, the message describes a danger using present simple or present continuous. After Information, the speaker gives factual status. After Question, the speaker uses a direct question form. After Answer, the speaker replies clearly. After Request, the speaker asks for help, permission, or service. After Intention, the speaker normally uses “I will” to state a future action.

  • Instruction + imperative: Instruction. Keep clear of the fairway.
  • Advice + should / recommended action: Advice. You should reduce speed in the traffic lane.
  • Warning + danger statement: Warning. You are running into danger.
  • Information + fact/status: Information. My vessel is restricted in her ability to manoeuvre.
  • Question + direct question: Question. What are your intentions?
  • Answer + direct reply: Answer. My present course is zero-nine-zero degrees.
  • Request + need/assistance/permission: Request. I require pilot assistance.
  • Intention + I will / I intend to: Intention. I will alter course to starboard.

Core Feature: The Message Marker Decision System

In real operations, a speaker has only a few seconds to choose the correct marker. A practical system is to identify the function of the message first, then choose the marker, then speak the details. This prevents mixed messages such as giving an order in the form of a casual question or reporting danger without clearly saying that it is a warning.

1. Identify the situation: routine report, danger, order, request, planned action, or question.

2. Choose one marker before speaking the main message.

3. Use short SMCP wording after the marker.

4. Avoid slang, jokes, local expressions, and long explanations on VHF.

5. For critical information, require read-back or confirmation.

6. Correct misunderstanding immediately using “Say again,” “Correction,” or a clear Question.

Common Student Mistake: Students often use Information for every message because it feels safe. However, “Information. Reduce speed” is incorrect because “Reduce speed” is an order. Use Instruction. Reduce speed. Another common mistake is using Question when actually making a Request. “Question. Give me a tug” is incorrect. Use Request. I require tug assistance.
