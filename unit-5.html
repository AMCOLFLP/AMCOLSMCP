Unit 5: Vessel-to-Vessel Collision Avoidance

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students should be able to:

  • Describe traffic situations using bearing, range, CPA, TCPA, course, and speed.
  • Use future forms and conditionals to state intentions and expected passing arrangements.
  • Ask another vessel’s intentions using standard SMCP phrases.
  • State avoiding action without replacing COLREG responsibilities.
  • Transform radar information into concise spoken traffic reports.
Why this matters on board: Collision avoidance communication supports decision-making but does not replace COLREGs. Students must learn to speak precisely while still understanding their legal navigational responsibilities.

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit develops students’ ability to describe traffic situations, translate radar information into spoken reports, clarify intentions, and communicate without replacing COLREG responsibilities.

Expanded Main Discussion: communication supports COLREGs, not replaces them

This unit must be taught with a strong safety principle: VHF communication can clarify intentions, but it cannot cancel the responsibilities required by the COLREGs. Students should use communication to share observed data, ask intentions, warn about risk, and state planned action. They must avoid making vague verbal agreements that create false security.

Students should develop the habit of translating radar and visual information into exact speech: bearing, range, CPA, TCPA, relative position, and action. Without this data, “You are close” is too weak to support safe decision-making.

3. Grammar Focus

Unit Grammar Target: future intentions with will, conditional clauses with if, radar description patterns, warnings, and exact comparative language.

Collision-avoidance communication must be precise. Students need grammar that describes observed facts, predicts risk, asks intentions, and states own action without ambiguity. A VHF exchange may support collision avoidance, but it must never replace COLREG duties or good seamanship.

3.1 Using will for clear intentions

Use will + base verb after Intention to state a planned manoeuvre. This is stronger and clearer than vague phrases such as “I am thinking to turn” or “maybe I go.”

PatternExampleOperational Purpose
Intention. I will + base verbIntention. I will alter course to starboard.Declares avoiding action.
Intention. I will pass...Intention. I will pass astern of you.Clarifies passing arrangement.
Intention. I will maintain...Intention. I will maintain course and speed.States stand-on action when appropriate.
Intention. I will reduce...Intention. I will reduce speed to eight knots.States speed action.

3.2 Conditional clauses for safe coordination

Use if + present simple, will + base verb to connect another vessel’s action with your intended action. Keep the sentence short and avoid creating informal agreements that conflict with COLREGs.

  • If you maintain course and speed, I will pass astern of you.
  • If you alter course to starboard, I will maintain my present course.
  • If risk of collision remains, I will reduce speed further.
  • If you cannot alter course, report your intentions.

Student note: Conditional statements are useful, but they must not sound like negotiation against the Rules of the Road. The safest action must still follow COLREGs.

3.3 Radar-to-speech grammar patterns

Radar information should be spoken in a fixed pattern: target position, bearing, range, CPA, TCPA, and risk. This helps the listener understand the traffic picture quickly.

Information TypeGrammar PatternExample
BearingTarget is on + relative bearingTarget is on my starboard bow.
RangeRange is + number + milesRange is three decimal five miles.
CPACPA is + distanceCPA is zero decimal three miles.
TCPATCPA is + timeTCPA is twelve minutes.
RiskBearing is steady and range is decreasingYour bearing is steady and range is decreasing.

3.4 Warning grammar

Warnings must state danger directly. Use present simple for a general danger and present continuous when the danger is developing now.

  • Warning. Risk of collision exists.
  • Warning. You are running into danger.
  • Warning. You are crossing the traffic lane.
  • Warning. Your vessel is standing into shallow water.

3.5 Avoiding ambiguous comparative words

Words such as near, far, soon, left, right, and a little are risky unless supported by exact data. Use measurable language whenever possible.

VaguePrecise
You are too close.Warning. CPA is zero decimal two miles.
Turn right a little.Instruction. Alter course to starboard ten degrees.
I will go behind.Intention. I will pass astern of you.
It will happen soon.TCPA is eight minutes.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Warning: used when risk of collision or danger exists.
  • Question: used to ask another vessel’s intentions.
  • Intention: used to declare own avoiding action.
  • Information: used to state observed traffic facts.
  • Advice: used when recommending a safer action.

5. Say This, Not That

Use the standard phrase in the middle column. The expressions in the last column may be understood in normal English, but they are too informal, vague, or risky for maritime operations.

7. Core Feature: Radar-to-Speech Translation and COLREG Awareness

Radar information must be converted into precise spoken messages. A good traffic report includes target identity if known, bearing, range, CPA, TCPA, movement, and intended action. For example: “Information. Target on starboard bow, bearing zero-four-five degrees, range four decimal zero miles, CPA zero decimal three miles in twelve minutes.” This is clearer than “that ship is close.” Students must remember that VHF communication supports, but does not replace, COLREG duties. Action should be early, substantial, and clear.

Radar-to-speech checklist

  1. Identify target vessel if known.
  2. State bearing and range.
  3. State CPA and TCPA if available.
  4. State risk using Warning if needed.
  5. Ask intentions or state your own action using Intention.
  6. Make early, substantial, and clear action according to COLREGs.

Conversation analysis

The conversation uses a safe pattern: identify the other vessel, give radar data, state warning, ask intentions, then read back the passing arrangement. Students should practise not only speaking their own intention, but also confirming the other vessel’s intention.

Why vague passing language is risky

“I will go behind you somehow” is unsafe because it does not identify exact action. “I will pass astern of you” is standard because the relationship between the two vessels is clear.

Marker logic for traffic risk

Use Information for radar facts, Warning for collision risk, Question for intentions, and Intention for your own planned action. The marker sequence often follows the operational thinking sequence: observe → warn → ask → act.

Additional Grammar Discussion: avoiding uncertain language

In close-quarters situations, uncertain grammar can be dangerous. Phrases such as “maybe I will turn” or “I think I go behind” are not operationally strong. Use will for clear intention and exact sea terms for the action.

Weak GrammarOperational ProblemImproved Sentence
I maybe turn right.Uncertain and uses non-maritime direction.Intention. I will alter course to starboard.
You are near me.No measurable data.Information. Your bearing is steady, range two decimal five miles.
What you do?Informal question form.Question. What are your intentions?

Vocabulary as a radar-to-speech system

Collision-avoidance vocabulary works as a sequence: identify the target, state bearing/range, assess CPA/TCPA, describe the situation, then state action. Students should practise speaking the radar picture aloud using measurable data.
