Unit 5: On-Board Operations - Mooring and Cargo

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit focuses on internal shipboard communication during mooring, unmooring, cargo handling, and deck coordination. Students learn to use closed-loop communication and read-backs to prevent accidents.

2. Vocabulary List

Study the terms below. Focus on meaning, pronunciation, and how each word is used during real maritime operations.

3. Grammar Focus

Passive voice and present perfect are useful for reporting operational status. In mooring and cargo reports, the condition of equipment often matters more than the person who performed the action. Examples: “All lines are made fast,” “The hatch covers are secured,” “The cargo hose has been connected,” and “The forward spring has been slackened.” Imperatives are used for direct orders: “Heave in,” “Slack away,” “Hold on,” and “Keep clear.”

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Instruction: used for direct deck and cargo orders.
  • Answer: used to acknowledge or read back orders.
  • Information: used to report status or completion.
  • Warning: used for snap-back zones, suspended loads, or unsafe conditions.
  • Request: used when asking permission to start or stop work.

5. Say This, Not That

Use the standard phrase in the middle column. The expressions in the last column may be understood in normal English, but they are too informal, vague, or risky for maritime operations.

SituationSay This - Standard SMCP StyleNot That - Colloquial / Risky English
Mooring orderInstruction. Heave in the forward spring.Pull the front rope.
Status reportInformation. All lines are made fast.We are tied up already.
Safety warningWarning. Keep clear of snap-back zone.Do not stand near the rope.
Cargo statusInformation. Hatch covers are secured.The lids are closed.
Read-backAnswer. Heaving in the forward spring.Okay, doing it.

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Bridge: Fore station, Bridge. Question. Are you ready to heave in the head line? Over.

Fore Station: Bridge, Fore station. Answer. Ready to heave in the head line. Over.

Bridge: Instruction. Heave in the head line. Slack away the forward spring. Over.

Fore Station: Answer. Heaving in the head line. Slacking away the forward spring. Over.

Fore Station: Warning. Crew are clear of snap-back zone. Information. Head line is tight. Over.

Bridge: Instruction. Hold on head line. Over.

Fore Station: Answer. Holding on head line. Information. Forward spring is slack. Out.

Cargo Control: Deck, Cargo Control. Question. Are hatch covers secured? Over.

Deck: Answer. Hatch covers are secured and checked. Out.

7. Core Feature: Closed-Loop Communication and Read-backs

Closed-loop communication has three stages: the sender gives a clear order, the receiver repeats the critical part, and the sender corrects or accepts the read-back. This prevents dangerous mistakes such as heaving the wrong line, starting a pump too early, or standing in an unsafe position. A weak reply such as “OK” is not enough for critical operations. A strong read-back repeats the action and object: “Slacking away the stern line,” “Stopping cargo pump number two,” or “Opening manifold valve number three.”
