Unit 1: VHF Fundamentals and Routine Traffic

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit teaches students how to establish contact, spell vessel information with the phonetic alphabet, conduct routine VHF exchanges, and perform a radio check using clear, standardized language. It is the foundation for safe communication with VTS, port control, pilot stations, and nearby vessels.

3. Grammar Focus

Imperatives and present simple are the main grammar points. Imperatives give short operational commands, such as “Stand by,” “Change to channel one-two,” and “Say again.” Present simple is used for facts, routine reports, and vessel particulars: “My draught is seven decimal five metres,” “I require pilot assistance,” and “The vessel carries containers.” Students should avoid long informal sentences and should speak in short message units.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Information: used to report facts such as position, draught, ETA, or traffic status.
  • Question: used to request specific information from VTS or another station.
  • Answer: used to give a direct response.
  • Request: used when asking permission or assistance.
  • Instruction: used by an authority such as VTS when giving an operational order.

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Vessel: Bangkok VTS, Bangkok VTS, this is Motor Vessel Ocean Pioneer, Ocean Pioneer. Call sign Hotel-Bravo-Seven-Two. Over.

VTS: Ocean Pioneer, this is Bangkok VTS. Send your message. Over.

Vessel: Information. I am entering the fairway from the south. My position is bearing one-eight-zero degrees, distance four decimal five miles from Buoy Bravo. My draught is seven decimal eight metres. Over.

VTS: Question. What is your ETA at the pilot station? Over.

Vessel: Answer. My ETA at the pilot station is one-six-three-zero local time. Request. Radio check on this channel. Over.

VTS: Answer. Readability is five. Instruction. Maintain listening watch on channel one-two. Report at Buoy Charlie. Over.

Vessel: Answer. Maintaining listening watch on channel one-two. Will report at Buoy Charlie. Out.

7. Core Feature: The Radio Check Protocol and 1-5 Readability Scale

A radio check must be short, professional, and made on an appropriate working channel. Do not occupy distress and safety channels for routine checks. The receiving station reports readability using a five-point scale: 1 = unreadable, 2 = readable now and then, 3 = readable with difficulty, 4 = readable, and 5 = perfectly readable. Students should avoid vague phrases such as “loud and clear” when a scale is required. A good radio check includes station called, station calling, the words “radio check,” and the response “readability is ...”.
