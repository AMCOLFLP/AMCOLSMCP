Expanded Main Discussion: markers as safety labels

Unit 1 ties the whole module together. A message marker is not decoration; it tells the listener how to interpret the sentence before the details arrive. This is essential when radio quality is poor, accents differ, or the situation is developing quickly.

Students should practise choosing markers by asking one question: “What is the purpose of my message?” If the purpose is to order, use Instruction. If the purpose is to warn, use Warning. If the purpose is to state future action, use Intention. This unit should be used repeatedly as a reference during all other units.

Focus: This unit helps students understand, choose, and use all eight IMO SMCP message markers in real maritime communication. The purpose is to make spoken messages clear, short, and safe during routine operations, port arrival, emergency response, collision avoidance, onboard work, and navigational warnings.

Student-Friendly Key Idea: A message marker tells the listener the purpose of your message before the details are spoken. It is like a safety label for communication. When the marker is correct, the listener immediately knows whether the message is an Instruction, Advice, Warning, Information, Question, Answer, Request, or Intention.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students should be able to:

  • Define and distinguish all eight SMCP message markers.
  • Choose the correct marker based on communication purpose.
  • Use the correct grammar pattern after each marker.
  • Correct common marker mistakes in VHF and onboard communication.
  • Produce an integrated maritime conversation using all eight markers accurately.
Why this matters on board: Message markers are the core safety labels of SMCP communication. When students master them, their speech becomes clearer, shorter, and safer across all maritime situations.

Course Position Note: This unit is now placed first because the eight message markers are the foundation used in every later SMCP situation. Students should master the purpose of each marker before moving to routine VHF, emergency messages, pilotage, collision avoidance, mooring, and warnings.

Grammar Focus: How Markers Control Sentence Structure

Unit Grammar Target: choosing the correct sentence pattern after each SMCP message marker and avoiding mixed-purpose messages.

The eight SMCP message markers are not just vocabulary words. Each marker tells the listener what kind of grammar and meaning will follow. When students choose the correct marker, they also choose the correct sentence pattern: an order, a recommendation, a warning, a fact, a question, a reply, a request, or a planned action.

1. Instruction: orders and required actions

After Instruction, use an imperative verb. The listener must know exactly what action is required. Do not phrase an instruction as a polite question because it may sound optional.

PatternExampleWhy It Works
Instruction. + base verbInstruction. Reduce speed to six knots.The action is direct and measurable.
Instruction. Do not + base verbInstruction. Do not enter the fairway.The prohibition is clear.
Instruction. Report + object/timeInstruction. Report when pilot is on board.The required report is specified.

2. Advice: recommendations for safe action

After Advice, use should, recommend, or a short recommended action. Advice is not the same as an order. It gives a safer course of action, especially when the speaker is warning or guiding another station.

  • Advice. You should keep clear of the traffic lane.
  • Advice. Navigate with caution.
  • Advice. Keep a sharp lookout.

3. Warning: danger statements

After Warning, state the danger immediately. Use present simple for general risk and present continuous for developing danger.

  • Warning. Risk of collision exists.
  • Warning. You are running into danger.
  • Warning. Visibility is restricted by fog.
  • Warning. You are standing into shallow water.

4. Information: facts, status, and observations

After Information, give a factual statement. Use present simple for stable facts, present continuous for current situations, past simple for completed events, and passive voice for status reports.

Tense/StructureExampleUse
Present simpleInformation. My draught is seven decimal five metres.Vessel particulars.
Present continuousInformation. I am reducing speed.Action happening now.
Past simpleInformation. Collision occurred at one-four-two-zero UTC.Completed event.
Passive voiceInformation. Hatch covers are secured.Operational status.

5. Question: direct requests for information

After Question, use a direct question. Ask one clear question at a time. Do not hide a request inside a question.

  • Question. What are your intentions?
  • Question. What is your present course?
  • Question. Are you ready to receive pilot?

Important difference: “What is your ETA?” is a Question. “I require pilot assistance” is a Request, not a question.

6. Answer: direct response and read-back

After Answer, give a complete reply or a read-back. Do not reply with only “OK” when safety-critical information is involved.

  • Answer. My ETA at pilot station is one-four-three-zero UTC.
  • Answer. Altering course to starboard and passing astern of you.
  • Answer. Pilot ladder is rigged on port side two metres above water.

7. Request: assistance, permission, or service

After Request, use I require..., I request permission..., or Please arrange... in formal operational style. The message should state exactly what is needed.

PurposePatternExample
AssistanceI require + nounRequest. I require tug assistance.
PermissionI request permission to + verbRequest. I request permission to enter fairway.
Emergency helpI require immediate + nounRequest. I require immediate medical assistance.

8. Intention: planned future action

After Intention, use I will + base verb or I intend to + base verb. In VHF communication, I will is often clearer and shorter.

  • Intention. I will alter course to port.
  • Intention. I will pass astern of you.
  • Intention. I will reduce speed to eight knots.
  • Intention. I intend to anchor in position...

How to Choose the Correct Marker

Decision QuestionBest MarkerStudent Tip
Do I need the other station to do something?Instruction or RequestUse Instruction if you have authority; use Request if you are asking for help, service, or permission.
Am I giving a recommendation, not a direct order?AdviceUse Advice when the action is recommended for safety but not compulsory from your authority.
Is there immediate or possible danger?WarningUse Warning before describing the danger clearly.
Am I reporting a fact, status, position, equipment condition, or observation?InformationUse Information to make the message factual and neutral.
Do I need specific information?QuestionUse Question before asking what, where, when, how many, or whether.
Am I replying to a question?AnswerUse Answer and give a direct, complete response.
Am I telling another vessel my planned future action?IntentionUse Intention before a planned course, speed, or operational action.

Sample Conversation: Port Approach with All 8 Markers

Audio Practice: Unit 1 Sample Conversation

Listen to the complete model conversation first. Then repeat each message and practise the eight SMCP markers with clear pauses.

Conversation Script

Vessel: Rayong VTS, Rayong VTS, this is Motor Vessel Pacific Trader, Pacific Trader. Call sign Papa-Tango-Five-Seven. Over.

VTS: Pacific Trader, this is Rayong VTS. Send your message. Over.

Vessel: Information. I am approaching Pilot Boarding Ground Alpha. My present course is one-eight-five degrees. My speed is one-two knots. Over.

VTS: Question. What is your maximum draught and ETA at the pilot station? Over.

Vessel: Answer. My maximum draught is eight decimal two metres. My ETA at the pilot station is one-four-three-zero local time. Over.

VTS: Warning. Dense outbound traffic is reported near Buoy Romeo-Two. Fishing vessels are crossing the fairway. Over.

VTS: Advice. You should reduce speed and keep to the starboard side of the fairway. Over.

Vessel: Intention. I will reduce speed to eight knots and keep to the starboard side of the fairway. Over.

Vessel: Request. I require one pilot and two tugs for berthing at Terminal Bravo. Over.

VTS: Instruction. Proceed to Pilot Boarding Ground Alpha. Maintain listening watch on channel one-two. Report when pilot is on board. Over.

Vessel: Answer. Proceeding to Pilot Boarding Ground Alpha, maintaining listening watch on channel one-two, and reporting when pilot is on board. Pacific Trader, out.

Core Feature: The Message Marker Decision System

In real operations, a speaker has only a few seconds to choose the correct marker. A practical system is to identify the function of the message first, then choose the marker, then speak the details. This prevents mixed messages such as giving an order in the form of a casual question or reporting danger without clearly saying that it is a warning.

1. Identify the situation: routine report, danger, order, request, planned action, or question.

2. Choose one marker before speaking the main message.

3. Use short SMCP wording after the marker.

4. Avoid slang, jokes, local expressions, and long explanations on VHF.

5. For critical information, require read-back or confirmation.

6. Correct misunderstanding immediately using “Say again,” “Correction,” or a clear Question.

Common Student Mistake: Students often use Information for every message because it feels safe. However, “Information. Reduce speed” is incorrect because “Reduce speed” is an order. Use Instruction. Reduce speed. Another common mistake is using Question when actually making a Request. “Question. Give me a tug” is incorrect. Use Request. I require tug assistance.

Conversation analysis

The sample conversation includes all eight markers in one port approach situation. Students should identify each marker and explain why it was used. This reinforces the link between marker, grammar pattern, and operational purpose.

Why marker errors create risk

When a request is phrased as a question or an instruction is phrased as advice, the listener may not understand the urgency or authority of the message. Standard marker use removes this uncertainty.

Marker choice as a decision tree

Before speaking, decide: Am I ordering, recommending, warning, reporting, asking, replying, requesting, or planning? The answer gives the marker. This decision tree should become automatic through repeated role-play.

Additional Grammar Discussion: avoiding mixed-purpose messages

A common student problem is mixing two purposes in one marker. For example, “Question. Send me a tug” is wrong because the speaker is not asking for information; the speaker needs assistance. The correct form is “Request. I require tug assistance.”

Students should also avoid using Information for every message. If the sentence tells another station to act, it is probably an Instruction. If it recommends action, it is Advice. If it describes danger, it is Warning.

Vocabulary for communication purpose

The vocabulary in this unit is abstract, but it controls real operations. Students should connect each word to an action: authority leads to instruction, hazard leads to warning, assistance leads to request, and planned manoeuvre leads to intention.
