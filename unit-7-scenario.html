7. Core Feature: NAVTEX Decoding

NAVTEX messages are concise and may contain abbreviations. Students must learn to identify the type of message, the affected area, the position, the time, the hazard, and the recommended action. Common abbreviations include UTC, N, S, E, W, NM, VIS, WND, SEA, SWELL, GALE, and TEMPO. A student should convert a message such as “NAV WARN: UNLIT BUOY PSN 13-05N 100-48E. KEEP CLR.” into spoken English: “Warning. Unlit buoy reported in position one-three degrees zero-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees four-eight minutes East. Advice. Keep clear.”

Interactive Exercises

NAVTEX decoding checklist

  1. Identify whether the message is weather, navigation, SAR, or general safety.
  2. Locate position, time, and affected area.
  3. Expand abbreviations accurately.
  4. Choose Warning, Information, and Advice in sequence.
  5. Do not add details not present in the original message.

Sample Scenario Section: Sample Scenario: NAVTEX Decoding, Weather Warning, and SECURITE Broadcast

Student Task: Read the operational scenario carefully. Then answer the comprehensive questions. Your answers should connect vocabulary, grammar, SMCP markers, operational procedure, and correct maritime phraseology from this unit.

At 1100 UTC, MV Siam Trader receives the following NAVTEX message while transiting the Gulf of Thailand: 'NAV WARN 148/26. UNLIT BUOY IN PSN 12-42N 100-55E. DRIFTING CONTAINER REP 8 NM SE OF BUOY CHARLIE. WND NE 30-35 KT. VIS TEMPO 1 NM IN HVY RA. KEEP CLR AND RPT SIGHTING.' The OOW must decode the abbreviations, identify the affected hazards, and brief the master.

The weather is deteriorating. The wind is veering to east-north-east, and visibility is temporarily reduced by heavy rain. The vessel is near a fishing area, so the bridge team increases lookout and reduces speed. The master decides to broadcast a SECURITE message to nearby vessels after confirming the position of the drifting container on radar was not possible.

The broadcast must convert NAVTEX abbreviations into clear spoken English, use Warning and Advice markers, state the position correctly, and avoid vague descriptions such as 'something is floating somewhere near the buoy.'

Teacher Notes / Expected Answer Points
  • Expected answers decode abbreviations accurately and convert coordinates into degrees and minutes.
  • Good SECURITE broadcasts include all stations, station identity, warning, position, hazard, advice, and out.
  • Students should distinguish navigational hazards from weather conditions.

How to answer the scenario questions

For this unit scenario, students should not copy long sentences from the passage. A strong answer should identify the operational problem, choose the correct SMCP marker, use the target grammar from Unit 7, and produce a safe standard phrase. Teachers can ask students to justify why they selected each marker.
