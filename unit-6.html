Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students should be able to:

  • Give and read back mooring orders using closed-loop communication.
  • Use passive voice and present perfect to report operational status.
  • Identify mooring lines, cargo equipment, and hazardous deck areas.
  • Use Warning for snap-back zones, suspended loads, and unsafe conditions.
  • Produce concise onboard communication between bridge, deck, and cargo control.
Why this matters on board: Mooring and cargo operations involve heavy loads, moving lines, winches, and time pressure. Clear read-backs and status reports directly reduce accident risk.

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit focuses on internal shipboard communication during mooring, unmooring, cargo handling, and deck coordination. Students learn to use closed-loop communication and read-backs to prevent accidents.

Expanded Main Discussion: internal communication as accident prevention

Mooring and cargo communication often happens under noise, time pressure, and physical risk. Students must understand that the purpose of language is not only to exchange information, but to control dangerous energy: moving lines, winches, tug pull, cargo pumps, cranes, and suspended loads.

The key habit in this unit is closed-loop communication. A command is not complete until the receiver reads it back correctly and the action/status is confirmed. This prevents errors such as heaving the wrong line, standing in a snap-back zone, or starting cargo operations before equipment is secured.

3. Grammar Focus

Unit Grammar Target: passive voice for status reports, present perfect for completed operations, imperatives for orders, and read-back grammar for closed-loop communication.

During mooring and cargo operations, communication is often internal: bridge to fore station, bridge to aft station, deck to cargo control, and officer to crew. Grammar must make it clear whether an order is being given, an action is in progress, or a task has been completed.

3.1 Passive voice for operational status

Use passive voice when the status of equipment, lines, cargo, or safety arrangements is more important than the person who did the action. This is common in official reports and onboard updates.

PatternExampleMeaning
is/are + past participleAll lines are made fast.The mooring lines are secured now.
is/are + past participleHatch covers are secured.The covers are closed and locked.
is/are + past participleThe manifold valve is closed.The valve status is safe/closed.
is/are not + past participleThe cargo hose is not connected.The operation is not ready.

3.2 Present perfect for completed operations

Use present perfect when an action has just been completed and the result is important now. This tense is useful when reporting readiness or completion.

  • The forward spring has been made fast.
  • The stern line has been slackened.
  • The cargo hose has been connected and checked.
  • The snap-back zone has been cleared.
  • The hatch covers have been secured.

Compare: “The crew secured the hatch covers” focuses on who did it. “The hatch covers have been secured” focuses on current safe status.

3.3 Imperatives for mooring and cargo orders

Orders must use the base verb and a clear object. Avoid “please,” “can you,” and “okay” in safety-critical commands.

OrderObjectFull SMCP-style Command
Heave inthe head lineInstruction. Heave in the head line.
Slack awaythe stern lineInstruction. Slack away the stern line.
Hold onthe forward springInstruction. Hold on the forward spring.
Keep clear ofsnap-back zoneWarning. Keep clear of snap-back zone.
Stopcargo pump number twoInstruction. Stop cargo pump number two.

3.4 Present continuous for action in progress

Use present continuous to report what is happening now. This is useful for read-backs and live operational updates.

  • Answer. Heaving in the head line.
  • Answer. Slacking away the stern line.
  • Information. Cargo loading is continuing at reduced rate.
  • Information. Stevedores are working in hold number two.

3.5 Closed-loop grammar: order, read-back, confirmation

A closed-loop exchange has a strong grammar pattern. The sender uses an imperative. The receiver repeats the action using present continuous or the exact order. The sender corrects if necessary.

StepSpeakerExample
1. OrderBridgeInstruction. Slack away the aft spring.
2. Read-backAft stationAnswer. Slacking away the aft spring.
3. StatusAft stationInformation. Aft spring is slack.
4. Correction if neededBridgeCorrection. Hold on the aft spring.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Instruction: used for direct deck and cargo orders.
  • Answer: used to acknowledge or read back orders.
  • Information: used to report status or completion.
  • Warning: used for snap-back zones, suspended loads, or unsafe conditions.
  • Request: used when asking permission to start or stop work.

5. Say This, Not That

Use the standard phrase in the middle column. The expressions in the last column may be understood in normal English, but they are too informal, vague, or risky for maritime operations.

7. Core Feature: Closed-Loop Communication and Read-backs

Closed-loop communication has three stages: the sender gives a clear order, the receiver repeats the critical part, and the sender corrects or accepts the read-back. This prevents dangerous mistakes such as heaving the wrong line, starting a pump too early, or standing in an unsafe position. A weak reply such as “OK” is not enough for critical operations. A strong read-back repeats the action and object: “Slacking away the stern line,” “Stopping cargo pump number two,” or “Opening manifold valve number three.”

Sample Scenario Section: Sample Scenario: Mooring, Cargo Status, Closed-Loop Communication, and Safety Read-Backs

Student Task: Read the operational scenario carefully. Then answer the comprehensive questions. Your answers should connect vocabulary, grammar, SMCP markers, operational procedure, and correct maritime phraseology from this unit.

MV Pacific Bridge is alongside Berth 6 preparing for departure after completing container cargo operations. The Chief Officer reports that hatch covers on Bays 12 to 24 have been secured, container lashings have been checked, and stevedores have left the working area. On the foredeck, the head line, forward spring, and breast line remain made fast. On the aft station, the stern line and aft spring are under slight tension due to wind pushing the vessel off the berth.

The bridge begins unmooring with tug assistance. The pilot orders the aft station to slack away the stern line, hold on the aft spring, and keep clear of the snap-back zone. The aft station replies only, 'OK, doing it.' The Chief Officer corrects the response and requires a proper read-back. During the operation, a deck rating reports that a heaving line is fouled near a bollard and that one shore line cannot be released safely until tension is reduced.

The bridge must use closed-loop communication, clear instructions, safety warnings, and passive status reports. The cargo status must also be reported formally before departure.

Closed-loop communication checklist

  1. Sender gives one clear order at a time.
  2. Receiver reads back the action and object.
  3. Sender corrects immediately if the read-back is wrong.
  4. Receiver reports completion or unsafe condition.
  5. All crew stay clear of hazards before tension or lifting begins.

Conversation analysis

The conversation demonstrates order → read-back → status report. Students should circle the action verbs and underline the objects. This helps them see whether the receiver repeated the safety-critical part of the instruction.

Why “OK” is not enough

In onboard operations, “OK” does not prove that the correct line, pump, valve, or station was understood. A safe read-back repeats the exact action and object: “Slacking away the stern line.”

Marker logic for onboard work

Use Instruction to give a command, Answer to read it back, Information to report status, and Warning for hazards such as snap-back zones or suspended loads.

Additional Grammar Discussion: action status vs. completed status

Students need to distinguish between an action happening now and a completed safe status. Present continuous is used during the action; passive voice or present perfect is used after completion.

SituationGrammarExample
Action happening nowpresent continuousHeaving in the head line.
Current safe statuspassive voiceAll lines are made fast.
Recently completed actionpresent perfect passiveThe cargo hose has been connected.
Unsafe statusnegative passiveThe manifold valve is not closed.

A strong operational report should make the status unmistakable: “Aft spring is slack” is clearer than “rope okay.”

Vocabulary as operational control

Terms such as heave in, slack away, hold on, and make fast are not general verbs. They describe specific physical actions. Students should practise them with objects: head line, stern line, forward spring, aft spring, breast line, cargo hose, manifold valve, and hatch cover.
