Engine Room Fire: Which Alert Should Be Used?

View full size

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Audio Practice: Unit 3 Sample Conversation

Listen to the complete emergency exchange. Focus on message priority, position, assistance required, and clear emergency sequencing.

Vessel: MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. This is Motor Vessel Eastern Star, Eastern Star, Eastern Star. Call sign Lima-Delta-Four-Nine. MMSI two-three-five-six-seven-eight-nine-zero-one.

Vessel: MAYDAY Eastern Star. Position one-three degrees two-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees three-eight minutes East.

Vessel: Information. I have fire in engine room. Information. Fire is not under control. Request. I require immediate assistance.

Vessel: Information. Twenty-two persons on board. Intention. I am preparing to abandon ship if fire spreads to accommodation. Over.

Coast Radio: Eastern Star, this is Bangkok Radio. Answer. MAYDAY received. Instruction. Activate EPIRB. Instruction. Muster crew at emergency stations. SAR assistance is proceeding. Over.

Vessel: Answer. EPIRB activated. Crew mustered at emergency stations. Out.

Conversation analysis

The model distress call repeats the priority signal and vessel name, gives position early, describes the nature of distress, states the assistance required, and gives persons on board. Students should identify these parts before trying to write their own emergency broadcast.

Listening and Pronunciation Drill: Emergency Broadcast Recognition Drill

Student task: Listen as your teacher or partner reads the script twice. On the first listening, identify the main situation. On the second listening, write the key information: station name, vessel name, marker used, position/course/time, and action required.

MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. This is Motor Vessel Eastern Star, Eastern Star, Eastern Star.

Position one-three degrees two-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees three-eight minutes East.

Information. I have fire in engine room. Request. I require immediate assistance.

Information. Twenty-two persons on board. Intention. I am preparing to abandon ship if fire spreads.

Pronunciation Focus

  • MAYDAY is repeated three times at the start of distress communication.
  • PAN-PAN is pronounced as “pan pan,” not “pan.”
  • SECURITE is used for safety information, especially warnings to all stations.
Pair Practice: Student A reads the transmission. Student B writes down the key operational details, then reads them back using SMCP style.
