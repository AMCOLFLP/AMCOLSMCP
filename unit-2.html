Unit 2: VHF Fundamentals and Routine Traffic

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students should be able to:

  • Establish and close a routine VHF exchange using correct station identification, “Over,” and “Out.”
  • Spell vessel names, call signs, and difficult words using the IMO phonetic alphabet.
  • Report position, ETA, draught, course, speed, and operational status in short SMCP-style message units.
  • Perform a professional radio check using the 1–5 readability scale.
  • Select appropriate markers for routine traffic: Information, Question, Answer, Request, and Instruction.
Why this matters on board: Routine VHF traffic is the first communication skill cadets need before they can handle pilotage, warnings, emergencies, or traffic situations. Clear routine communication reduces channel congestion and prevents wrong assumptions.

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit teaches students how to establish contact, spell vessel information with the phonetic alphabet, conduct routine VHF exchanges, and perform a radio check using clear, standardized language. It is the foundation for safe communication with VTS, port control, pilot stations, and nearby vessels.

Expanded Main Discussion: From casual speech to operational radio language

Routine VHF traffic seems simple, but it trains the habits used in every higher-risk situation later in the course. Students should understand that a routine call is not ordinary conversation. It is a controlled exchange where every part has a purpose: identifying the station called, identifying the station calling, stating the message function, giving exact information, and closing correctly.

In real operations, weak routine communication can create unnecessary channel traffic, wrong vessel identification, missed reports, or delayed instructions from VTS. For this reason, students should practise speaking slowly, reading numbers as separate digits, spelling call signs with the phonetic alphabet, and separating one idea from the next. A good routine report should be short enough to repeat and complete enough for the listener to act on.

3. Grammar Focus

Unit Grammar Target: imperatives, present simple reporting, question forms, short answers, and standard VHF openings/closings.

Routine VHF communication must be short, predictable, and easy to repeat. In Unit 2, grammar is not used to make long polite conversation; it is used to make the operational purpose clear. Students should learn to speak in small message units: call the station, identify the vessel, state the marker, give the message, and close with Over or Out.

3.1 Imperatives for VHF control

The imperative is the command form of the verb. It begins with the base verb and normally has no subject. In SMCP-style communication, imperatives are common because they reduce unnecessary words and make instructions easy to hear.

PatternMaritime ExampleUse
Base verb + objectChange to channel one-two.Moving to a working channel.
Base verb + adverb/prepositionSpeak slowly.Improving radio clarity.
Do not + base verbDo not use channel one-six for routine traffic.Giving a prohibition.
Stand byStand by on channel one-two.Telling another station to wait and listen.

Student note: In normal English, students may say “Can you please wait?” In radio communication, Stand by is clearer and faster. A routine VHF message should avoid long introductions such as “I would like to ask you if...” Use Question and ask directly.

3.2 Present simple for vessel facts and routine reports

The present simple is used for stable facts, repeated procedures, and vessel particulars. It is useful when reporting identity, draught, cargo type, route, equipment status, or normal operations.

  • Fact: My vessel carries containers.
  • Particular: My maximum draught is seven decimal eight metres.
  • Routine: We maintain listening watch on channel one-six.
  • Equipment status: AIS is operational.

Do not use present continuous for permanent vessel data. Say “My call sign is Hotel-Bravo-Seven-Two,” not “My call sign is being Hotel-Bravo-Seven-Two.”

3.3 Asking and answering routine questions

Questions on VHF should request one exact piece of information. Avoid asking several questions at once. Start with Question when the purpose may not be clear.

FunctionCorrect FormExample
Ask identityWhat is your...?Question. What is your call sign?
Ask timeWhat is your ETA...?Question. What is your ETA at pilot station?
Ask positionWhat is your position?Question. What is your present position?
Ask permission/serviceMay I / I request...Request. I request permission to enter fairway.

3.4 Over, Out, and message endings

Over means “my transmission is finished and I expect your reply.” Out means “the exchange is finished and I do not expect a reply.” Students must not say “Over and out” because the two words give opposite instructions.

  • Reply expected: Bangkok VTS, this is Ocean Pioneer. Question. What is my berth number? Over.
  • No reply expected: Ocean Pioneer, maintaining listening watch on channel one-two. Out.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Information: used to report facts such as position, draught, ETA, or traffic status.
  • Question: used to request specific information from VTS or another station.
  • Answer: used to give a direct response.
  • Request: used when asking permission or assistance.
  • Instruction: used by an authority such as VTS when giving an operational order.

7. Core Feature: The Radio Check Protocol and 1-5 Readability Scale

A radio check must be short, professional, and made on an appropriate working channel. Do not occupy distress and safety channels for routine checks. The receiving station reports readability using a five-point scale: 1 = unreadable, 2 = readable now and then, 3 = readable with difficulty, 4 = readable, and 5 = perfectly readable. Students should avoid vague phrases such as “loud and clear” when a scale is required. A good radio check includes station called, station calling, the words “radio check,” and the response “readability is ...”.

Sample Scenario Section: Sample Scenario: Routine VHF Contact, Radio Check, and VTS Reporting

Student Task: Read the operational scenario carefully. Then answer the comprehensive questions. Your answers should connect vocabulary, grammar, SMCP markers, operational procedure, and correct maritime phraseology from this unit.

MV Andaman Spirit, a general cargo vessel, is approaching Rayong traffic area in daylight. The vessel has just altered course to enter the recommended fairway. The Third Officer must contact Rayong VTS before passing Buoy R-2. The vessel's call sign is Hotel-Juliett-Four-Three, her MMSI is 567234981, her maximum draught is 6.8 metres, her air draft is 31 metres, and her ETA at the pilot boarding ground is 1530 local time. Visibility is good, but several fishing boats are reported near the eastern edge of the fairway.

The Third Officer first calls VTS on channel one-six and is instructed to change to channel one-two. After changing channel, the Third Officer makes a radio check because the previous transmission included static. VTS replies that readability is four. VTS then asks for the vessel's present position, course, speed, draught, and ETA. The officer must report clearly, use the phonetic alphabet when spelling the call sign, and avoid casual language such as 'I can hear you fine' or 'we will arrive around half past three'.

Rayong VTS later instructs the vessel to maintain listening watch on channel one-two, keep to the starboard side of the fairway, and report again when abeam Buoy R-4. The Third Officer must read back the important instructions and close the communication correctly.

Operational radio check steps

  1. Select an appropriate working channel; do not block distress traffic.
  2. Call the station clearly and identify your own vessel.
  3. State: “Request. Radio check on this channel.”
  4. Receive the readability rating from one to five.
  5. If readability is poor, speak slower, change position/channel if instructed, or repeat critical data.

Conversation analysis

Notice how the vessel gives one group of facts at a time: position, draught, ETA, then request for radio check. VTS replies with the readability rating and then gives an instruction. The vessel closes with a read-back, not just “OK.” This is the model students should imitate in their own VHF scripts.

Why the standard phrases are safer

Routine phrases are repeated thousands of times in port approaches. Standard words such as “Say again,” “Stand by,” and “Readability is five” reduce accent problems and prevent the listener from guessing. Students should practise replacing friendly classroom English with short operational language.

Marker decision practice for routine traffic

In this unit, the most common confusion is between Question and Request. Use Question when you need information: “What is my berth?” Use Request when you need service, permission, or assistance: “I request permission to enter fairway.”

Additional Grammar Discussion: message-unit structure

A routine VHF message should be built as several short message units rather than one long sentence. This improves clarity and gives the receiving station time to write important details. The recommended sequence is: station called + station calling + marker + message + closing word.

Message UnitExampleGrammar Point
Station calledBangkok VTS, Bangkok VTSName repeated for attention.
Station callingThis is MV Ocean PioneerPresent simple identity phrase.
MarkerInformation.Signals the function.
Main messageMy present course is one-eight-zero degrees.Present simple for facts.
ClosingOver.Reply expected.

Students should avoid combining requests and questions in one sentence. Say “Request. Radio check on this channel. Over.” Then wait for the answer. This is safer than adding several questions after the radio check.

How to master this vocabulary

Do not memorize the words only as definitions. Practise them as report-building blocks. For example, draught, ETA, call sign, course, and speed should be combined into one clear VTS report. Students should also learn which words must be spelled and which numbers must be read digit by digit.

  • Identity frame: This is Motor Vessel ___, call sign ___.
  • Position frame: My position is bearing ___ degrees, distance ___ miles from ___.
  • Status frame: My draught is ___ metres. My ETA is ___ local time.
