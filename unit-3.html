Unit 3: Pilotage and Port Arrival

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit prepares students for port approach communication, pilot boarding arrangements, helm orders, engine orders, and safe coordination between bridge, pilot station, and deck crew.

2. Vocabulary List

Study the terms below. Focus on meaning, pronunciation, and how each word is used during real maritime operations.

3. Grammar Focus

Prepositions of place and movement are necessary for pilotage: “on the port side,” “alongside the berth,” “to the pilot station,” “one metre above the water,” “under the bridge,” and “ahead of the buoy.” Imperatives are also frequent in helm and engine orders. Students must use exact direction words: port, starboard, ahead, astern, midships, and degrees. Avoid “left” and “right” in formal navigation orders.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Instruction: used by pilots and bridge officers to give helm, engine, and boarding orders.
  • Question: used to ask boarding time, side, height, or tug requirements.
  • Answer: used to confirm arrangements.
  • Intention: used to state planned manoeuvre.
  • Information: used to report readiness or vessel condition.

5. Say This, Not That

Use the standard phrase in the middle column. The expressions in the last column may be understood in normal English, but they are too informal, vague, or risky for maritime operations.

6. Sample Conversation

Practise this conversation aloud. Pay attention to message markers, call signs, numbers, read-backs, and the words “Over” and “Out.”

Vessel: Manila Pilot Station, this is Motor Vessel Sea Orchid. Call sign November-Kilo-Six-Five. Over.

Pilot Station: Sea Orchid, this is Manila Pilot Station. Question. What is your ETA at pilot boarding ground? Over.

Vessel: Answer. My ETA is zero-six-three-zero local time. Information. My present draught is eight decimal two metres. Request. Pilot boarding instructions. Over.

Pilot Station: Instruction. Proceed to pilot boarding ground Alpha. Instruction. Rig pilot ladder on starboard side, two metres above water. Advice. Make a lee for pilot boat. Over.

Vessel: Answer. Proceeding to pilot boarding ground Alpha. Pilot ladder will be rigged on starboard side two metres above water. I will make a lee for pilot boat. Out.

Pilot: Bridge, Pilot. Instruction. Port ten.

Helmsman: Port ten.

Pilot: Instruction. Midships.

Helmsman: Midships.

Pilot: Instruction. Dead slow ahead.

OOW: Dead slow ahead.

7. Core Feature: Standard Helm and Engine Orders

Helm and engine orders must be exact, short, and read back. A correct order-reply pattern prevents wrong-way turns, engine mistakes, and delayed reactions. Helm orders include “Port ten,” “Starboard twenty,” “Hard-a-port,” “Hard-a-starboard,” “Midships,” “Steady,” and “Steer zero-nine-zero.” Engine orders include “Dead slow ahead,” “Slow ahead,” “Half ahead,” “Full ahead,” “Stop engine,” “Dead slow astern,” and “Full astern.” The helmsman or officer repeats the order exactly. When the order is carried out, a confirmation may be given, such as “Course zero-nine-zero steadied.”
