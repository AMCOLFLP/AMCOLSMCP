Unit 3: Distress, Urgency, and Safety Communications

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students should be able to:

  • Distinguish clearly between MAYDAY, PAN-PAN, and SECURITE situations.
  • Construct a complete distress or urgency message with identity, position, nature of problem, assistance required, and persons on board.
  • Use modals of obligation and capability such as must, must not, can, cannot, and require in emergency messages.
  • Apply Warning, Request, Information, Instruction, and Answer accurately under pressure.
  • Recognize when communication priority must override routine radio traffic.
Why this matters on board: Emergency communication must be fast, complete, and unambiguous. Students must learn not only phrases, but also how to organize life-saving information in the correct order.

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit trains students to recognize and produce priority communications during emergencies. Students learn the difference between distress, urgency, and safety messages and practise organizing critical information under pressure.

Expanded Main Discussion: priority before language

In an emergency, students must first classify the situation correctly before choosing words. A fire that threatens the vessel, uncontrolled flooding, collision with danger to life, or abandonment requires distress communication. A machinery failure, medical case, or steering problem may begin as urgency if there is no immediate grave danger. A hazard broadcast to other ships is usually safety traffic.

Students should learn to organize emergency messages in a fixed order. The order is important because rescue stations listen for identity, position, nature of emergency, assistance required, and persons on board. Emotional language wastes time; structured information saves time.

PriorityWhen UsedTypical Marker Combination
MAYDAYGrave and imminent danger.Information + Request + Instruction/Answer.
PAN-PANUrgent safety problem, not yet distress.Information + Request.
SECURITESafety or navigation warning.Warning + Information + Advice.

3. Grammar Focus

Unit Grammar Target: modals of obligation, ability, prohibition, emergency requests, present continuous for developing emergencies, and clear emergency sequencing.

Emergency communication requires direct grammar. The listener must quickly understand what is happening, where it is happening, how serious it is, and what assistance is required. In Unit 3, grammar should help students give life-saving information in the correct order, not create long or emotional sentences.

3.1 Modals of ability and inability: can / cannot

Use can and cannot to describe the vessel’s capability during an emergency. In SMCP-style communication, cannot is preferred over the contracted form can’t because it is clearer on radio.

MeaningPatternMaritime Example
Abilitycan + base verbI can proceed at reduced speed.
Inabilitycannot + base verbI cannot control flooding.
Navigation limitationcannot + manoeuvre/steer/proceedI cannot steer. I am not under command.
Communication problemcannot + receive/readI cannot receive your transmission clearly.

3.2 Modals of obligation and prohibition: must / must not

Use must for safety duties that are compulsory. Use must not for actions that are prohibited because they may endanger life, vessel, cargo, or environment.

  • Instruction. Crew must proceed to muster stations.
  • Warning. Personnel must not enter the engine room.
  • Instruction. Activate EPIRB immediately.
  • Information. Fire team must use breathing apparatus.

Students should not use weak words such as “maybe,” “try to,” or “if possible” when the action is compulsory. In emergencies, uncertainty can delay response.

3.3 Require, request, and need in emergency messages

For assistance, SMCP-style messages often use I require... after the marker Request. This is stronger and clearer than informal “We need help.”

PurposeStandard PatternExample
Immediate rescueRequest. I require immediate assistance.Used in MAYDAY traffic.
Medical helpRequest. I require medical assistance.Often used in PAN-PAN medical traffic.
TowingRequest. I require tug assistance.Used when disabled or unable to manoeuvre safely.
EvacuationRequest. I require medical evacuation.Used when person must be taken ashore or to hospital.

3.4 Present continuous for developing emergencies

Use present continuous to show an emergency is happening now or is getting worse. This is useful for fire, flooding, listing, drifting, or sinking.

  • Information. I am taking water in cargo hold number two.
  • Information. Fire is spreading to accommodation.
  • Warning. Vessel is drifting towards shallow water.
  • Information. I am listing to port.

3.5 Recommended emergency message order

A complete emergency message should follow a predictable order. This helps rescue units collect critical details quickly.

  1. Priority signal: MAYDAY, PAN-PAN, or SECURITE.
  2. Vessel identity: name, call sign, MMSI if available.
  3. Position: latitude/longitude or bearing and distance from a known point.
  4. Nature of emergency: fire, flooding, collision, medical case, machinery failure.
  5. Assistance required: immediate assistance, medical assistance, tug assistance, SAR assistance.
  6. Persons on board and other useful information.
  7. Closing word: Over, if a reply is expected.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Warning: signals danger and urgency.
  • Request: asks for assistance or evacuation.
  • Information: reports position, nature of distress, and persons on board.
  • Instruction: gives life-saving orders.
  • Answer: confirms receipt and response.

7. Core Feature: The 3-Tier Alert System - MAYDAY, PAN-PAN, SECURITE

The priority of a maritime message must match the situation. MAYDAY is used only for distress: grave and imminent danger to life or the vessel. PAN-PAN is used for urgency: a serious situation affecting safety but not immediately life-threatening. SECURITE is used for safety: warnings about navigation or weather. The wrong priority can delay rescue, overload emergency channels, or create confusion. A complete distress message should include: distress signal, vessel identity, position, nature of distress, assistance required, persons on board, other useful information, and “Over.”

Procedure checklist for alert selection

  1. Ask: Is there grave and imminent danger? If yes, use MAYDAY.
  2. Ask: Is safety threatened but not immediately life-threatening? If yes, use PAN-PAN.
  3. Ask: Am I warning other stations about navigation or weather? If yes, use SECURITE.
  4. State position before long explanations.
  5. State assistance required clearly and repeat if necessary.

Conversation analysis

The model distress call repeats the priority signal and vessel name, gives position early, describes the nature of distress, states the assistance required, and gives persons on board. Students should identify these parts before trying to write their own emergency broadcast.

Why emotional language is unsafe

Phrases such as “Help us quickly” may be understandable, but they do not provide enough operational information. Standard phrases tell rescue units the exact need: immediate assistance, medical assistance, tug assistance, or evacuation.

Marker logic under pressure

In emergency traffic, Information gives facts, Request asks for assistance, Warning identifies danger, and Instruction gives life-saving orders. Students should never use Question when they need help immediately.

Additional Grammar Discussion: emergency clarity and escalation

Emergency grammar should show whether the situation is stable, developing, or worsening. Use present simple for status, present continuous for developing danger, and past simple for completed events.

  • Status: My main engine is disabled.
  • Developing danger: Fire is spreading to accommodation.
  • Completed event: Collision occurred at zero-four-two-zero UTC.

When a PAN-PAN develops into a MAYDAY, the grammar must change from “I require assistance” to stronger, immediate distress wording: “MAYDAY. Information. I am in grave and imminent danger. Request. I require immediate assistance.” Students should understand escalation, not just memorize separate messages.

Using emergency vocabulary accurately

Emergency terms should not be replaced with casual classroom words. Foundering, listing, not under command, medical evacuation, and immediate assistance have operational meanings. Students should practise saying what the vessel can or cannot do: “I cannot steer,” “I cannot control flooding,” or “I can proceed at reduced speed.”
