2. Vocabulary List

Study the terms below. Focus on meaning, pronunciation, and how each word is used during real maritime operations.

TermStudent-Friendly MeaningHow It Is Used On Board
MAYDAYDistress signal used when a vessel or person is in grave and imminent danger.Used for sinking, serious fire, collision with danger to life, or abandoning ship.
PAN-PANUrgency signal used when safety is threatened but there is no immediate grave danger.Used for engine failure, medical assistance, or lost steering when danger is not immediate.
SECURITESafety signal used for important navigational or meteorological warnings.Used for hazards, drifting containers, severe weather warnings, or navigation warnings.
GMDSSGlobal Maritime Distress and Safety System.Includes DSC, EPIRB, SART, NAVTEX, and emergency radio procedures.
DSCDigital Selective Calling.May be used to send a digital distress alert before voice communication.
EPIRBEmergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon.Transmits distress position to search and rescue systems.
SARTSearch and Rescue Transponder.Helps rescuers locate survival craft on radar.
SARSearch and Rescue.Operations to locate and assist persons or vessels in distress.
FounderingTaking water and in danger of sinking.Reported as nature of distress.
ListingLeaning to port or starboard.Can indicate flooding, cargo shift, or stability problem.
Abandon shipOrder to leave the vessel.Used when remaining on board is unsafe.
Muster stationAssigned assembly area during emergency.Crew and passengers proceed there when alarm sounds.
Fire in engine roomEmergency involving fire in machinery space.Requires exact location and assistance needed.
Not under commandVessel unable to manoeuvre as required by the rules.Reported when steering or propulsion failure prevents normal action.
Immediate assistanceHelp required without delay.Used in distress messages.
Persons on boardNumber of people aboard.Required in distress information.
Medical evacuationRemoval of sick or injured person for treatment.Often associated with PAN-PAN medical messages.
PositionLatitude/longitude or bearing and distance from known point.One of the most important parts of emergency communication.

Using emergency vocabulary accurately

Emergency terms should not be replaced with casual classroom words. Foundering, listing, not under command, medical evacuation, and immediate assistance have operational meanings. Students should practise saying what the vessel can or cannot do: “I cannot steer,” “I cannot control flooding,” or “I can proceed at reduced speed.”
