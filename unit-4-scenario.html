7. Core Feature: Standard Helm and Engine Orders

Helm and engine orders must be exact, short, and read back. A correct order-reply pattern prevents wrong-way turns, engine mistakes, and delayed reactions. Helm orders include “Port ten,” “Starboard twenty,” “Hard-a-port,” “Hard-a-starboard,” “Midships,” “Steady,” and “Steer zero-nine-zero.” Engine orders include “Dead slow ahead,” “Slow ahead,” “Half ahead,” “Full ahead,” “Stop engine,” “Dead slow astern,” and “Full astern.” The helmsman or officer repeats the order exactly. When the order is carried out, a confirmation may be given, such as “Course zero-nine-zero steadied.”

Interactive Exercises

Safe pilotage communication checklist

  1. Confirm pilot boarding time and position.
  2. Confirm ladder side and height above water.
  3. Read back all helm and engine orders exactly.
  4. Report readiness of pilot ladder, mooring stations, and tugs.
  5. Use standard direction terms only: port, starboard, ahead, astern.

Sample Scenario Section: Sample Scenario: Pilot Boarding, Port Arrival, Helm Orders, and Tug Coordination

Student Task: Read the operational scenario carefully. Then answer the comprehensive questions. Your answers should connect vocabulary, grammar, SMCP markers, operational procedure, and correct maritime phraseology from this unit.

MV Golden Horizon is inbound for Terminal Bravo with a maximum draught of 8.4 metres and an LOA of 186 metres. The vessel is approaching the pilot boarding ground at 0630 local time. Wind is from the north-east, Force 5, with a short swell on the starboard bow. Port Control instructs the vessel to rig the pilot ladder on the port side, two metres above the water, with manropes ready and a heaving line available. Because the vessel has a high freeboard, the master asks whether a combination ladder is required.

After the pilot boards, the bridge team prepares for a narrow-channel transit. The pilot gives several helm and engine orders: 'Starboard ten,' 'Midships,' 'Steady on zero-seven-five,' 'Dead slow ahead,' and later 'Stop engine.' Each order must be repeated exactly by the helmsman or officer. Two tugs are assigned: one forward and one aft. The pilot also advises the master to make a lee for the pilot boat during boarding and to maintain a safe under-keel clearance during the approach.

During the final approach, the fore and aft mooring stations report readiness. The bridge must coordinate port arrival communication, pilot instructions, tug information, and safe order read-backs.

How to answer the scenario questions

For this unit scenario, students should not copy long sentences from the passage. A strong answer should identify the operational problem, choose the correct SMCP marker, use the target grammar from Unit 4, and produce a safe standard phrase. Teachers can ask students to justify why they selected each marker.
