Unit 4: Pilotage and Port Arrival

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students should be able to:

  • Request and confirm pilot boarding time, location, side, and ladder height.
  • Use prepositions of place and movement accurately in pilotage instructions.
  • Recognize and read back standard helm and engine orders.
  • Use Instruction, Question, Answer, Intention, Advice, and Information during port arrival.
  • Convert informal port arrival language into standard SMCP phrases.
Why this matters on board: Pilotage communication is time-sensitive and happens in restricted waters. Small language errors involving side, height, speed, or course can create serious safety risks.

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit prepares students for port approach communication, pilot boarding arrangements, helm orders, engine orders, and safe coordination between bridge, pilot station, and deck crew.

Expanded Main Discussion: precision during restricted-water operations

Pilotage and port arrival communication requires exact language because the vessel is often close to shallow water, traffic lanes, berth structures, tugs, pilot boats, and mooring teams. Students must recognize that small grammar words become safety-critical: on starboard side, two metres above water, to pilot station, and alongside the berth all describe different operational relationships.

The main learning goal is not only to know pilotage vocabulary, but to produce clear arrangements and read them back correctly. The listener should never have to guess the side, height, time, or instruction.

3. Grammar Focus

Unit Grammar Target: prepositions of place and movement, imperative helm/engine orders, future arrangements, and read-back grammar during pilotage.

Pilotage communication happens in restricted waters where there is little time for unclear language. Grammar must help students describe exact positions, movements, side, height, direction, and readiness. In this unit, small words such as on, to, above, alongside, ahead, and astern are operationally important.

3.1 Prepositions of place for pilot boarding

Use prepositions of place to say where equipment, vessel, pilot boat, tug, or berth is located. A wrong preposition can create an unsafe boarding arrangement.

PrepositionMeaning in PilotageExample
onside or surfaceRig pilot ladder on starboard side.
abovehigher than a reference pointPilot ladder two metres above water.
alongsidenext to the vessel or berthPilot boat is alongside on port side.
atexact place or timeReport at pilot boarding ground Alpha.
underbelow somethingMaintain safe under-keel clearance.
betweenin the middle of two pointsKeep vessel between Buoy Alpha and Buoy Bravo.

3.2 Prepositions of movement for port approach

Use prepositions of movement to describe how the vessel moves in relation to the fairway, buoy, berth, or pilot station.

  • to: Proceed to pilot boarding ground Alpha.
  • from: I am approaching from the south.
  • into: Enter the fairway with caution.
  • out of: Keep out of the traffic lane.
  • towards: Vessel is moving towards berth number three.
  • past: Pass Buoy Charlie on your port side.

3.3 Imperatives for helm and engine orders

Helm and engine orders use short imperative forms. They do not need polite phrases, explanations, or extra subjects. The receiver must repeat the order exactly.

Order TypeOrderCorrect Read-back
HelmPort ten.Port ten.
HelmMidships.Midships.
CourseSteer zero-nine-zero.Steering zero-nine-zero.
EngineDead slow ahead.Dead slow ahead.
EngineStop engine.Stop engine.

Student note: Do not convert helm orders into casual English. “Turn left a little” is not acceptable. Use Port ten or Starboard ten depending on the required rudder angle.

3.4 Future arrangements and intentions

Port arrival often requires future arrangements. Use will for planned action and be going to only when informal planning is acceptable in classroom practice. In SMCP-style VHF, will after Intention is clearer.

  • Intention. I will make a lee on port side.
  • Intention. I will reduce speed to six knots.
  • Answer. Pilot ladder will be rigged on starboard side.
  • Information. Tug will be made fast forward.

3.5 Reporting readiness

Readiness reports are usually short. They often use ready to + base verb or passive status forms.

PatternExampleUse
Ready to + verbFore station ready to let go anchor.Crew can start when ordered.
Equipment + is/are + past participlePilot ladder is rigged.Equipment status is complete.
Line/tug + made fastForward tug is made fast.Mooring/tug connection is secured.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Instruction: used by pilots and bridge officers to give helm, engine, and boarding orders.
  • Question: used to ask boarding time, side, height, or tug requirements.
  • Answer: used to confirm arrangements.
  • Intention: used to state planned manoeuvre.
  • Information: used to report readiness or vessel condition.

7. Core Feature: Standard Helm and Engine Orders

Helm and engine orders must be exact, short, and read back. A correct order-reply pattern prevents wrong-way turns, engine mistakes, and delayed reactions. Helm orders include “Port ten,” “Starboard twenty,” “Hard-a-port,” “Hard-a-starboard,” “Midships,” “Steady,” and “Steer zero-nine-zero.” Engine orders include “Dead slow ahead,” “Slow ahead,” “Half ahead,” “Full ahead,” “Stop engine,” “Dead slow astern,” and “Full astern.” The helmsman or officer repeats the order exactly. When the order is carried out, a confirmation may be given, such as “Course zero-nine-zero steadied.”

Sample Scenario Section: Sample Scenario: Pilot Boarding, Port Arrival, Helm Orders, and Tug Coordination

MV Golden Horizon is inbound for Terminal Bravo with a maximum draught of 8.4 metres and an LOA of 186 metres. The vessel is approaching the pilot boarding ground at 0630 local time. Wind is from the north-east, Force 5, with a short swell on the starboard bow. Port Control instructs the vessel to rig the pilot ladder on the port side, two metres above the water, with manropes ready and a heaving line available. Because the vessel has a high freeboard, the master asks whether a combination ladder is required.

After the pilot boards, the bridge team prepares for a narrow-channel transit. The pilot gives several helm and engine orders: 'Starboard ten,' 'Midships,' 'Steady on zero-seven-five,' 'Dead slow ahead,' and later 'Stop engine.' Each order must be repeated exactly by the helmsman or officer. Two tugs are assigned: one forward and one aft. The pilot also advises the master to make a lee for the pilot boat during boarding and to maintain a safe under-keel clearance during the approach.

During the final approach, the fore and aft mooring stations report readiness. The bridge must coordinate port arrival communication, pilot instructions, tug information, and safe order read-backs.

Safe pilotage communication checklist

  1. Confirm pilot boarding time and position.
  2. Confirm ladder side and height above water.
  3. Read back all helm and engine orders exactly.
  4. Report readiness of pilot ladder, mooring stations, and tugs.
  5. Use standard direction terms only: port, starboard, ahead, astern.

Conversation analysis

The sample exchange demonstrates a complete cycle: the vessel requests instructions, the pilot station gives location, side, and height, and the vessel reads back the instruction. The helm and engine orders at the end show how short command forms are used during manoeuvring.

Why port/starboard language matters

Students should avoid “left” and “right” because they depend on viewpoint. Port and starboard are fixed to the vessel. This is especially important for pilot ladder rigging, tug positions, and helm orders.

Marker logic for pilotage

Pilot stations and pilots often use Instruction for required actions. Vessels use Question to ask for time or location, Request to ask for pilotage service, and Answer to read back arrangements.

Additional Grammar Discussion: side, height, and movement

Many pilotage errors come from weak prepositions. Students must practise three layers of meaning: location, movement, and measurement.

  • Location: Pilot boat is alongside on port side.
  • Movement: Proceed to pilot boarding ground Alpha.
  • Measurement: Rig the ladder two metres above water.

Read-back grammar should repeat all safety-critical details. “Pilot ladder ready” is incomplete. “Pilot ladder is rigged on starboard side two metres above water” is a safe status report because it includes equipment, side, and height.

Vocabulary as port-arrival instructions

Students should group vocabulary into functions: pilot boarding equipment, bridge orders, tug/mooring terms, and port approach locations. This helps them build practical messages instead of memorizing isolated words.
