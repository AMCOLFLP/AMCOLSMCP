7. Core Feature: The 3-Tier Alert System - MAYDAY, PAN-PAN, SECURITE

The priority of a maritime message must match the situation. MAYDAY is used only for distress: grave and imminent danger to life or the vessel. PAN-PAN is used for urgency: a serious situation affecting safety but not immediately life-threatening. SECURITE is used for safety: warnings about navigation or weather. The wrong priority can delay rescue, overload emergency channels, or create confusion. A complete distress message should include: distress signal, vessel identity, position, nature of distress, assistance required, persons on board, other useful information, and “Over.”

Interactive Exercises

Procedure checklist for alert selection

  1. Ask: Is there grave and imminent danger? If yes, use MAYDAY.
  2. Ask: Is safety threatened but not immediately life-threatening? If yes, use PAN-PAN.
  3. Ask: Am I warning other stations about navigation or weather? If yes, use SECURITE.
  4. State position before long explanations.
  5. State assistance required clearly and repeat if necessary.

Sample Scenario Section: Sample Scenario: Escalating Emergency from PAN-PAN to MAYDAY

Student Task: Read the operational scenario carefully. Then answer the comprehensive questions. Your answers should connect vocabulary, grammar, SMCP markers, operational procedure, and correct maritime phraseology from this unit.

MV Eastern Guardian is proceeding 18 nautical miles south-west of Koh Chang when an engine-room fuel leak is reported during the 0400-0800 watch. The main engine is stopped as a precaution, and the vessel begins drifting slowly to the north-east. At first, there is no fire, the weather is moderate, and the master decides to transmit a PAN-PAN message requesting technical assistance and warning nearby vessels that the ship is temporarily not under command.

Twenty minutes later, smoke is detected near the fuel purifier, and the fixed fire detection panel indicates fire in the engine room. The engine-room team withdraws, the space is sealed, and CO2 release is prepared. The vessel has 24 persons on board. Position is Latitude one-two degrees one-eight minutes North, Longitude one-zero-zero degrees zero-five minutes East. The master decides that the situation has become grave and imminent because the fire may spread and propulsion is unavailable.

The bridge team must upgrade the communication priority, transmit the correct distress message, include all essential information, and use clear SMCP markers. Nearby vessels must be warned to keep clear, and the crew must be instructed to muster at emergency stations.

Teacher Notes / Expected Answer Points
  • Students should distinguish urgency from distress and recognize escalation.
  • Good answers include MAYDAY repeated three times, vessel identity, position, nature of distress, assistance required, persons on board, and other useful information.
  • Accept grammar using must, must not, cannot, require, and imperative emergency orders.

How to answer the scenario questions

For this unit scenario, students should not copy long sentences from the passage. A strong answer should identify the operational problem, choose the correct SMCP marker, use the target grammar from Unit 3, and produce a safe standard phrase. Teachers can ask students to justify why they selected each marker.
