Unit 7: Meteorological and Navigational Warnings

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students should be able to:

  • Decode basic NAVTEX-style abbreviations into clear spoken English.
  • Describe weather and navigational hazards using precise adjectives and passive structures.
  • Broadcast a SECURITE safety message with position, hazard, and advice.
  • Differentiate between factual Information, danger Warning, and safe-action Advice.
  • Read positions, times, wind force, visibility, and sea-state information accurately.
Why this matters on board: NAVTEX and weather warnings are often abbreviated. Students must learn how to convert short written warnings into clear spoken communication without changing the operational meaning.

1. Unit Title and Focus

This unit teaches students how to understand weather and navigational warnings, decode NAVTEX-style information, and convert abbreviated warnings into clear spoken SMCP messages.

Expanded Main Discussion: decoding information without changing meaning

NAVTEX and maritime safety information are often short, abbreviated, and data-heavy. Students must learn to expand them into spoken English while preserving the original meaning. They should not guess missing information or exaggerate the warning.

The main skill is conversion: abbreviation → hazard type → position/time → movement or weather details → advice. A correct spoken warning gives navigators enough information to assess risk and take safe action.

3. Grammar Focus

Unit Grammar Target: adjectives and adverbs of intensity, passive warning structures, abbreviated NAVTEX-to-full-sentence conversion, and position/time reporting.

Meteorological and navigational warnings must convert compact data into clear operational English. Students should learn to describe conditions accurately, keep the original meaning of the warning, and avoid exaggeration or vague language.

3.1 Adjectives for weather and sea condition

Adjectives describe the state or intensity of wind, sea, swell, visibility, and precipitation. Use standard maritime terms instead of casual words such as “bad,” “big,” or “terrible.”

CategoryUseful AdjectivesExample
Visibilitygood, moderate, poor, restrictedVisibility is restricted by fog.
Sea statesmooth, slight, moderate, rough, very roughSea state is rough.
Windlight, moderate, strong, gale-force, severeGale-force wind is expected.
Rainlight, moderate, heavy, severeHeavy rain is reducing visibility.
Hazarddrifting, unlit, submerged, unchartedDrifting hazard reported in position...

3.2 Adverbs for change and intensity

Adverbs describe how weather or conditions change. They help the listener understand speed, area, duration, and seriousness.

  • rapidly: Wind is rapidly increasing.
  • gradually: Visibility is gradually improving.
  • locally: Locally poor visibility in rain showers.
  • temporarily: Visibility temporarily reduced by squalls.
  • approximately: Container is drifting south-east at approximately two knots.

3.3 Passive structures in warnings

Passive voice is common because the source of the report is often less important than the hazard itself. Use passive forms to report hazards, outages, and observations.

PatternExampleUse
Hazard + reportedDrifting container reported in position...Hazard was observed/reported by someone.
Visibility + reduced/restrictedVisibility restricted by fog.Weather affects navigation.
Light + extinguishedBuoy light is extinguished.A navigation aid is not functioning.
Area + affectedEastern approach is affected by heavy swell.Warns vessels about an area.

3.4 NAVTEX abbreviation to spoken English

Students must expand abbreviations without changing meaning. Do not guess missing information. Keep the hazard, position, time, and advice.

NAVTEX StyleSpoken SMCP-style Version
VIS POOR IN FOGWarning. Visibility poor in fog.
UNLIT BUOY PSN 13-05N 100-48EWarning. Unlit buoy reported in position one-three degrees zero-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees four-eight minutes East.
GALE WARNING AREA ALPHAWarning. Gale warning for area Alpha.
KEEP CLRAdvice. Keep clear.

3.5 Position and time grammar

Positions and times should be read systematically. Do not use approximate local descriptions such as “near here” or “around morning” when exact information is available.

  • Position: Position one-three degrees zero-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees four-eight minutes East.
  • Time: Reported at one-two-zero-zero UTC.
  • Range and bearing: Hazard is bearing zero-eight-five degrees, distance three decimal zero miles from Buoy Charlie.
  • Movement: Container is drifting south-east at approximately two knots.

3.6 Common grammar problems in Unit 7

Casual weatherThe weather is very bad.Warning. Gale-force wind and rough sea expected.

4. SMCP Marker Application

The following message markers are especially useful in this unit:

  • Warning: used for hazards and dangerous weather.
  • Information: used to report factual weather or navigation details.
  • Advice: used to recommend safe action.
  • Instruction: used by authorities when an action is required.
  • Question: used to clarify unclear warnings or positions.

5. Say This, Not That

Use the standard phrase in the middle column. The expressions in the last column may be understood in normal English, but they are too informal, vague, or risky for maritime operations.

SituationSay This - Standard SMCP StyleNot That - Colloquial / Risky English
Fog warningWarning. Visibility restricted by fog.It is very foggy.
Floating objectWarning. Drifting hazard reported in position...There is something floating somewhere.
Weather updateInformation. Wind veering north-west, Force eight.The wind is changing direction.
Safety adviceAdvice. Keep a sharp lookout.Please watch carefully.
Buoy problemInformation. Buoy light is extinguished.The buoy lamp is broken.

7. Core Feature: NAVTEX Decoding

NAVTEX messages are concise and may contain abbreviations. Students must learn to identify the type of message, the affected area, the position, the time, the hazard, and the recommended action. Common abbreviations include UTC, N, S, E, W, NM, VIS, WND, SEA, SWELL, GALE, and TEMPO. A student should convert a message such as “NAV WARN: UNLIT BUOY PSN 13-05N 100-48E. KEEP CLR.” into spoken English: “Warning. Unlit buoy reported in position one-three degrees zero-five minutes North, one-zero-zero degrees four-eight minutes East. Advice. Keep clear.”

NAVTEX decoding checklist

  1. Identify whether the message is weather, navigation, SAR, or general safety.
  2. Locate position, time, and affected area.
  3. Expand abbreviations accurately.
  4. Choose Warning, Information, and Advice in sequence.
  5. Do not add details not present in the original message.

Conversation analysis

The SECURITE message starts by calling all stations, names the coast radio station, gives the hazard position, reports movement, and gives advice. Students should identify which details are warning, information, and advice.

Why position is essential

“Something is floating somewhere” cannot support navigation. A useful warning gives the object, position, movement if known, time reported, and advice. Students should practise reading coordinates until they can do it without hesitation.

Marker logic for warnings

Warning introduces the hazard, Information gives supporting facts, and Advice tells vessels what safe action is recommended. This three-part sequence is ideal for SECURITE broadcasts.

Additional Grammar Discussion: passive warnings and controlled adjectives

In warnings, passive voice allows the hazard to come first. This is useful because the listener needs to know the danger before knowing who reported it. Students should use controlled adjectives instead of emotional descriptions.

  • Weak: The sea is really bad.
  • Better: Sea state is rough.
  • Weak: Somebody says a buoy light is broken.
  • Better: Buoy light is extinguished.

When decoding NAVTEX, students should preserve time and position exactly. If the message says “TEMPO VIS POOR,” the spoken version should be “Visibility temporarily poor,” not “Visibility always poor.”

Vocabulary as warning categories

Vocabulary should be grouped into weather terms, navigation hazards, positions/times, and advice phrases. This helps students decode messages quickly and prepare a SECURITE broadcast.
