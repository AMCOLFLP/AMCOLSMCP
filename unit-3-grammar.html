3. Grammar Focus

Unit Grammar Target: modals of obligation, ability, prohibition, emergency requests, present continuous for developing emergencies, and clear emergency sequencing.

Emergency communication requires direct grammar. The listener must quickly understand what is happening, where it is happening, how serious it is, and what assistance is required. In Unit 3, grammar should help students give life-saving information in the correct order, not create long or emotional sentences.

3.1 Modals of ability and inability: can / cannot

Use can and cannot to describe the vessel’s capability during an emergency. In SMCP-style communication, cannot is preferred over the contracted form can’t because it is clearer on radio.

MeaningPatternMaritime Example
Abilitycan + base verbI can proceed at reduced speed.
Inabilitycannot + base verbI cannot control flooding.
Navigation limitationcannot + manoeuvre/steer/proceedI cannot steer. I am not under command.
Communication problemcannot + receive/readI cannot receive your transmission clearly.

3.2 Modals of obligation and prohibition: must / must not

Use must for safety duties that are compulsory. Use must not for actions that are prohibited because they may endanger life, vessel, cargo, or environment.

  • Instruction. Crew must proceed to muster stations.
  • Warning. Personnel must not enter the engine room.
  • Instruction. Activate EPIRB immediately.
  • Information. Fire team must use breathing apparatus.

Students should not use weak words such as “maybe,” “try to,” or “if possible” when the action is compulsory. In emergencies, uncertainty can delay response.

3.3 Require, request, and need in emergency messages

For assistance, SMCP-style messages often use I require... after the marker Request. This is stronger and clearer than informal “We need help.”

PurposeStandard PatternExample
Immediate rescueRequest. I require immediate assistance.Used in MAYDAY traffic.
Medical helpRequest. I require medical assistance.Often used in PAN-PAN medical traffic.
TowingRequest. I require tug assistance.Used when disabled or unable to manoeuvre safely.
EvacuationRequest. I require medical evacuation.Used when person must be taken ashore or to hospital.

3.4 Present continuous for developing emergencies

Use present continuous to show an emergency is happening now or is getting worse. This is useful for fire, flooding, listing, drifting, or sinking.

  • Information. I am taking water in cargo hold number two.
  • Information. Fire is spreading to accommodation.
  • Warning. Vessel is drifting towards shallow water.
  • Information. I am listing to port.

3.5 Recommended emergency message order

A complete emergency message should follow a predictable order. This helps rescue units collect critical details quickly.

  1. Priority signal: MAYDAY, PAN-PAN, or SECURITE.
  2. Vessel identity: name, call sign, MMSI if available.
  3. Position: latitude/longitude or bearing and distance from a known point.
  4. Nature of emergency: fire, flooding, collision, medical case, machinery failure.
  5. Assistance required: immediate assistance, medical assistance, tug assistance, SAR assistance.
  6. Persons on board and other useful information.
  7. Closing word: Over, if a reply is expected.

3.6 Common grammar problems in Unit 3

ProblemWeak SentenceImproved Emergency Sentence
Contraction unclear on radioI can’t steer.Information. I cannot steer.
Too emotionalPlease help us, everything is bad.Request. I require immediate assistance.
Missing positionWe are on fire near the coast.Information. I have fire in engine room. Position is...
Wrong priorityMAYDAY for fog warning.Use SECURITE for navigational or meteorological warnings.

Additional Grammar Discussion: emergency clarity and escalation

Emergency grammar should show whether the situation is stable, developing, or worsening. Use present simple for status, present continuous for developing danger, and past simple for completed events.

  • Status: My main engine is disabled.
  • Developing danger: Fire is spreading to accommodation.
  • Completed event: Collision occurred at zero-four-two-zero UTC.

When a PAN-PAN develops into a MAYDAY, the grammar must change from “I require assistance” to stronger, immediate distress wording: “MAYDAY. Information. I am in grave and imminent danger. Request. I require immediate assistance.” Students should understand escalation, not just memorize separate messages.
