SMCP Marker Application: All 8 Markers Explained

MarkerMain FunctionUseful Grammar PatternStandard Example
InstructionThe speaker gives a required action.Imperative verb: Alter course; Keep clear; Report position.Instruction. Alter course to starboard.
AdviceThe speaker recommends the safest action, but it may not be a direct order.Should / recommend / advise: You should reduce speed.Advice. You should keep clear of the fairway.
WarningThe speaker reports danger that may lead to accident or loss.Danger phrase + consequence: You are running into danger.Warning. You are running into danger.
InformationThe speaker gives a fact, status, condition, or report.Present simple / present continuous / past simple facts.Information. My vessel is not under command.
QuestionThe speaker asks for specific information.Wh-question / yes-no question: What are your intentions?Question. What is your present course?
AnswerThe speaker gives a direct response to a previous question.Short direct response; no unnecessary explanation.Answer. My present course is one-eight-zero degrees.
RequestThe speaker asks for permission, assistance, service, or action.I require / request / please arrange in formal style.Request. I require tug assistance.
IntentionThe speaker states a planned future action.I will + base verb; I intend to + base verb.Intention. I will alter course to port.
Instructor Note: Students should not memorize the markers only as vocabulary. They must learn when to choose each marker. The best test is this question: “What is the purpose of my message?”

Marker choice as a decision tree

Before speaking, decide: Am I ordering, recommending, warning, reporting, asking, replying, requesting, or planning? The answer gives the marker. This decision tree should become automatic through repeated role-play.

How to Choose the Correct Marker

Decision QuestionBest MarkerStudent Tip
Do I need the other station to do something?Instruction or RequestUse Instruction if you have authority; use Request if you are asking for help, service, or permission.
Am I giving a recommendation, not a direct order?AdviceUse Advice when the action is recommended for safety but not compulsory from your authority.
Is there immediate or possible danger?WarningUse Warning before describing the danger clearly.
Am I reporting a fact, status, position, equipment condition, or observation?InformationUse Information to make the message factual and neutral.
Do I need specific information?QuestionUse Question before asking what, where, when, how many, or whether.
Am I replying to a question?AnswerUse Answer and give a direct, complete response.
Am I telling another vessel my planned future action?IntentionUse Intention before a planned course, speed, or operational action.

Say This, Not That

Communication PurposeSay This - Standard SMCP StyleNot That - Informal / Risky English
Giving an orderInstruction. Reduce speed to six knots.Can you slow down a little?
Giving a recommendationAdvice. You should keep clear of the traffic lane.Better go away from there.
Reporting dangerWarning. You are running into shallow water.Be careful, it is dangerous there.
Giving factual statusInformation. My main engine is disabled.Our engine has a problem.
Asking about actionQuestion. What are your intentions?What are you doing now?
Giving a replyAnswer. I will maintain present course and speed.Okay, I just keep going.
Asking for helpRequest. I require immediate medical assistance.We need a doctor fast.
Stating a planned maneuverIntention. I will pass astern of you.I am going behind you.

Why marker errors create risk

When a request is phrased as a question or an instruction is phrased as advice, the listener may not understand the urgency or authority of the message. Standard marker use removes this uncertainty.
