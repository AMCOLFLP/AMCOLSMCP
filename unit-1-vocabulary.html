Vocabulary List: Key Terms for Message Markers

TermStudent-Friendly MeaningHow It Is Used On Board
Message markerA word placed before a message to show its purpose.Used before the main message, such as “Warning. You are running into danger.”
Communication purposeThe reason for speaking: to order, ask, warn, report, reply, or request.Helps the speaker select the correct SMCP marker.
Operational orderA command connected to vessel safety or ship operation.Usually introduced by Instruction.
RecommendationSuggested safe action, not always a direct command.Usually introduced by Advice.
HazardA danger to navigation, people, cargo, vessel, or environment.Usually introduced by Warning.
Factual reportA clear statement of status, position, observation, or condition.Usually introduced by Information.
Specific inquiryA direct question asking for exact information.Usually introduced by Question.
Direct responseA clear reply to a question.Usually introduced by Answer.
AssistanceHelp needed from another station, vessel, port, tug, pilot, or rescue unit.Usually introduced by Request.
Planned maneuverAn action the vessel plans to take.Usually introduced by Intention.
Read-backRepeating critical information to confirm correct understanding.Used after important instructions, helm orders, and safety messages.
Closed-loop communicationA communication cycle where message, read-back, and confirmation are completed.Reduces mistakes during high-risk operations.
AmbiguityUnclear meaning that may cause misunderstanding.Avoided by using markers and standard phrases.
AuthorityThe right to give an operational instruction.VTS, pilot, master, or officer may use Instruction depending on context.
ComplianceFollowing an order, rule, or procedure.A vessel may say “I will comply” or “I cannot comply.”
ClarificationMaking a message clear when it was not understood.Use “Say again” or ask a Question.
PriorityThe level of urgency or importance of a message.Warning and Request may become urgent depending on danger.
Standard wordingApproved maritime language that avoids slang and local expressions.Used in VHF and onboard communication.

Vocabulary for communication purpose

The vocabulary in this unit is abstract, but it controls real operations. Students should connect each word to an action: authority leads to instruction, hazard leads to warning, assistance leads to request, and planned manoeuvre leads to intention.
